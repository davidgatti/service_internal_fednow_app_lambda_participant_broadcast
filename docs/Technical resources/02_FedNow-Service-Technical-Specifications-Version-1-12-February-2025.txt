Overview of FedNow Service Functionality
The FedNow Service is an interbank 24x7x365 real-time gross settlement (RTGS) service with integrated
clearing functionality. The service enables financial institutions (FIs) to deliver end-to-end instant
payments to their customers.
Like other payment and settlement services offered by the Federal Reserve Banks, the FedNow Service
settles obligations between financial institutions through debits and credits to balances in their or their
correspondent’s master accounts with the Federal Reserve Banks. With the FedNow Service, end
customers can send and receive payments any time, any day, anywhere, and have full access to those
funds immediately.

FedNow Service Sample Message Flow: Customer Credit
Transfer
The FedNow Service enables transactions to clear and settle in real time, allowing financial institutions
(FIs) of all sizes to enable their customers to instantly send and receive money.
Figure 1 provides a step-by-step overview of the FedNow Service payment process in which the Federal
Reserve Banks settle and execute and the Receiver FI accepts a customer payment message.
For this sample flow, the Sender FI or Receiver FI may either send and receive messages to and from the
service or designate a Service Provider to act as its agent (e.g., a processor, core solution or other
payment hub) to perform those functions on its behalf. A participating FI may settle using its own Federal
Reserve Bank master account or it may designate a correspondent for that purpose.
Refer to the FedNow Service Operating Procedures for additional details on the customer credit transfer
flow, participation types and settlement.

Note: Additional information on the messaging workflows supported by the FedNow Service can be found
in the FedNow Service ISO 20022 Message Flows guide on the SWIFT MyStandards site.

The following table describes each numbered step in Figure 1.
Step

Description

1

Initiation: The sender (an individual or business) initiates a payment with their FI through an end-user
interface outside of the FedNow Service. The FedNow Service Sender FI is responsible for validating the
payment according to its internal processes and requirements.

2

The Sender FI submits a payment message (ISO message pacs.008) to the FedNow Service.

3

The FedNow Service validates the payment message — for example, by verifying that the message meets
proper format specifications and complies with applicable controls.

4*

The FedNow Service sends the contents of the payment message to the recipient’s FI to seek
confirmation that the Receiver FI intends to accept the payment message. At this point, the Receiver FI
determines how to handle the message (accept, reject or accept without posting (ACWP)). Among other
things, the Receiver FI should use this step to determine whether it maintains an account for the recipient
identified in the contents of the payment message.

5*

Confirmation: In this example flow, the Receiver FI sends a positive response of “accept” to the FedNow
Service, confirming it intends to accept the payment message.

6

The FedNow Service settles the payment, debiting and crediting the designated master accounts of the
Sender FI and Receiver FI (or of their correspondents), respectively. Steps 2-6 are expected to complete
within a few seconds but will take no more than 20 seconds.

7

The FedNow Service sends an advice to the Receiver FI and an acknowledgement to the Sender FI,
executing the payment order and notifying each that the Federal Reserve Banks settled the payment
message. Correspondents enabled within a FedNow profile may choose to receive a notification of
debit/credit entries (ISO message camt.054).
The Receiver FI matches the advice of credit to the pacs.008 ISO message and then makes funds
available to the recipient.

8

In communicating with the recipient, the Receiver FI should notify its customer immediately following
receipt of an advice and may use any reasonable means of communication consistent with their customer
agreements, including standard channels for which the recipient is enabled.
As a best practice, upon receipt of an acknowledgment (pacs.002), the Sender FI should notify the end
customer of a successful or unsuccessful transaction.

9

Confirmation of posting: In general, the Receiver FI has the option of sending a message through the
FedNow Service to the Sender FI indicating that the payment has been posted to the recipient’s account.
If the Receiver FI sends a confirmation of posting message through the service, the Sender FI should
notify its customer that the funds have been made available to the recipient.

10

The FedNow Service limits processing of messages to within 20 seconds and settles within this set
amount of time or not at all. While the Federal Reserve Banks expect most payment messages to settle in
a few seconds, well below the maximum allowable limit, the payment timeout clock sets expectations for
FIs that transactions submitted to the service are settled or rejected almost immediately.

*Steps 4 and 5 are intended to reduce the number of misdirected payments and resulting exception cases that can occur in
high-volume systems.

FedNow Service Network Architecture
The FedNow Service, like the other Federal Reserve payment systems, is accessible via communication
channels provided by the Federal Reserve Banks’ FedLine Solutions. Access to the FedNow Service is
available over a dedicated wide area network (WAN) or a virtual private network (VPN) connection over
the internet.
Participating financial institutions have various options to connect with the service — including connecting
directly with Federal Reserve Bank Services using their own FedLine Solutions or connecting via a
Service Provider.
Participants exchange messages with the FedNow Service using an IBM® MQ client. Participants use an
IBM MQ client library to connect remotely to FedNow MQ servers over the FedLine Solutions. The
FedNow MQ environment operates as a single cluster across sites, allowing Participants to connect to a
server at any site.

FedNow Service Profiles and Components
To understand the messaging flows and connectivity requirements of the FedNow Service, it is important
to first understand the design model elements. This section describes the model entities that form the
participation and connectivity model.
Refer to the FedNow Service Operating Procedures for more details about the terms in this section.

Participant Profile
A Participant Profile is required for each RTN a financial institution uses to send and/or receive payments
and messaging through the FedNow Service, regardless of whether they connect to the FedNow Service
directly or via Service Provider(s). Each RTN is associated to a single Participant Profile, and there can
be multiple Participant Profiles per institution. The Participant Profile defines the settings and features in
the FedNow Service that are enabled for each RTN.

Authorized Connection Profile
The Authorized Connection Profile (ACP) represents an entity (such as a financial institution or Service
Provider), which establishes connectivity to the FedNow Service, enabling one or more Participants to
access the application-to-application or user-to-application functions.

Connection Party
A Connection Party is a conceptual entity that owns connectivity to the FedNow Service and is authorized
to send and receive messages, as well as log in to the FedNow interface.
A Connection Party is mapped to one or more Participant Profiles. Each mapping carries specific
permissions that control which functions the connection profile can execute on behalf of the Participant
Profile.
The Connection Party ID defaults to the RTN or Electronic Transaction Identifier (ETI) of the Participant or
Service Provider. This identifier is carried in the To and From elements of the Business Application
Header (BAH) of all ISO messages exchanged with the FedNow Service. A Connection Party also owns
the endpoints (queues) used to exchange messages with the FedNow Service. If the Connection Party ID
needs to be changed, contact the Support Center.

Connection Point
A Connection Point is a logical grouping of endpoints (queues) and is owned by a Connection Party. The
Connection Point is intended to represent a site or location from which a Participant connects to the
FedNow Service. Consequently, a Connection Party can own one or more Connection Points. A
Connection Point holds a set of endpoints (queues) over which the Connection Party exchanges
messages with the FedNow Service. Therefore, the identifier of the Connection Point
(7-character ID assigned by the FedNow Service) is part of endpoint names.

MQ Overview
IBM MQ (messaging and queuing) is a solution that allows applications to communicate with each other
in real-time by exchanging data across multiple platforms and environments. Messages are sent and
received via queues, which allow applications to run independently (asynchronously).
When a participant payment application wants to conduct a transaction with another application—in this
case, the FedNow® Service—it creates an ISO 20022 compliant message and puts the message onto a
queue. The queue may be on the same MQ queue manager or on other connected MQ queue managers.
The queue manager works to reliably transfer the message from the originating queue manager to target
queue managers.
Applications do not communicate directly with each other; MQ queue managers do. Once the message is
safely on a queue, the sending application is free to move on to other important transactions. The MQ
messaging infrastructure optimizes message flow and application efficiency. Further, MQ supports
applications that run in different frameworks, platforms, languages, clouds and locations and ensures
streamlined communication for all applications, while remaining fully integrated.

How the FedNow Service Works with MQ
In order to communicate with the FedNow Service using IBM MQ, we support connectivity over
FedLine connections (for more information, see FedLine Solutions). The FedNow Service supports
asynchronous messaging in a point-to-point (application-to-application) flow as shown below.

Figure 3. FedNow MQ Connectivity
The FedNow Service hosts a cluster of IBM MQ queue managers where messaging resources such as
queues are configured. The payment applications operated by participants (or their service providers)
interact directly with FedNow MQ queue managers to exchange messages using an MQ client with an
embedded MQ client library, which must be located on the server running the payment application.
•

MQ client. Enables payment applications to issue MQI calls to FedNow MQ queue managers.
The MQ Client allows the application to put or get messages on or off queues. The MQ client also
receives the output from the call and passes it back to the sending application.

•

MQ client library. Facilitates the remote connection to FedNow MQ servers and enables the
exchange of data between applications, systems, services and files.

Queue Configuration
Queues used to communicate with the FedNow Service are hosted by the FedNow queue managers.
FedNow queue managers impose a set of rules governing the configuration of queues.

IN Queues
Queues that deliver messages from the FedNow Service to Participants use a naming pattern that allows
for customization by the Participant. Association of queue to message categories is determined by the
Participant.
The naming pattern for queues carrying messages from the FedNow Service to Participants is:
<connection point id>.IN.<customer defined>
IN represents the direction of the message flow, which is incoming from the perspective of the

FedNow Service
Replies

FedNow Service reply queues carry replies to messages sent by the Participant.
Messages on FedNow Service reply queues are either notifications of
transactions where the Participant is on the receiving side, or broadcasts and
reports sent by the FedNow Service to the Participant.

Other Incoming
Messages

Other incoming message queues carry messages that are not replies.

The Participant who owns a Connection Point needs to assign a FedNow Service reply queue for each

Maximum Queue Depth
The FedNow Service controls the maximum queue depth, currently 1,000 messages, which is the
maximum number of messages that can be present on a queue. When the max queue depth is reached,
message delivery to the queue ceases and any further messages destined for that queue are not
delivered.
Similarly, regardless of the fill status of the queue, messages not retrieved within their time-to-live period
are automatically removed from the queue. The receiving Participant must make use of reconciliation
reports and message retrieval functions to obtain any messages that were missed due to these
conditions.
For this reason, the FedNow Service expects connected Participants to always maintain active listeners
for all queues, except when they are explicitly signed off or disconnected. When a Participant is signed
off, messages other than pacs.008 and pacs.004 continue to be delivered to their queues until the max
queue depth is reached.

Application Design Considerations
The use of MQ Cluster functionality by the FedNow Service has certain important implications for client
applications, which need to be considered when architecting the solution:
•

Specifically targeting messages by message id, correlation id or any other message selector will
not work reliably. If the connection making the request is attached to a different queue manager
than the one that hosts the message, the message will not be found.

•

Consumers need to be long running and stable so that the cluster can properly identify where the
consumers are active and direct the messages accordingly.

For a client consumer to work seamlessly with the FedNow MQ cluster, it must be a long running process
that uses the same connection and queue handle to continuously issue timed GET requests (without a
message selector). Failure to do so may result in the inability to consume messages from the FedNowhosted queues. Setting a ‘long’ timeout on the GET call to retrieve messages is important to avoid
overloading the servers. ‘Long’ is of course relative but we would recommend timeouts of 10 seconds or
more.
Refer to Note on MDBs and Activation Specifications In Appendix A, for important information on using
Message Driven Beans and Activation Specifications.
For performance reasons, it is also highly recommended for processes that PUT messages onto the
queues use persistent, pooled connections. Participants are allowed to have multiple, concurrent
connections open (a maximum of 200 in the Production environment at this time) and are expected
to make use of multiple connections concurrently to meet their performance needs.
It is recommended for Participants to consider solution designs that decouple the processing of
messages received from the FedNow MQ servers by creating a layer that quickly pulls the messages off
of the FedNow queues and stores them locally onto a messaging, streaming or other storage solution,
from which the actual business processing can occur.
This approach would provide increased flexibility for processing, as messages can be further distributed
by type or other criteria in ways that are not supported by the FedNow queues. It would also ensure that
FedNow queues are quickly drained even under heavy load, avoiding possible stoppage of message
delivery due to full queues.

Resiliency
The FedNow Service is designed to be highly resilient in a way that is transparent to the connected
Participants. Client systems are not expected to take any specific action to enable resumption of
operations if a FedNow Service component failure occurs. While the FedNow Service operates at multiple
locations, separated by large distances, it appears to the client as a single target for connectivity.
Connected clients do not need to be aware of the physical location to which they are connected.
Resiliency for the FedNow Service is achieved through multiple levels of redundancy at all tiers between
the client application and the FedNow Service application itself. A failure of a component along the
communication path results in a dropped connection, but the client should be able to immediately
reconnect — connection requests are routed by surviving components to one of the FedNow queue
managers.
The FedNow MQ clustering setup uses follow-the-reader functionality, which ensures that when the
consumer and producer are connected to different queue managers, the messages find their way to the
consumer.
For client applications to take advantage of FedNow Service resiliency, clients must enable retry
capabilities. As mentioned earlier in Application Design Considerations, processes that consume
messages should be long-running and continuously issuing GET requests. Such processes should
handle any connection-lost errors and attempt to reconnect until successful. Similarly, processes that
PUT messages should handle connection-lost errors and retry. When retrying, it is important to discard
the broken connection and attempt to establish a new one.

Connection Point Affinity
Connection Point Affinity is a feature of the FedNow Service meant to help connected Participants meet
their resiliency requirements. Some Participants may operate their solutions at two or more locations,
which are either disconnected from each other or replicated on a lag. In such scenarios, it is important
that all messages related to one transaction be processed at the same location.
Connection Points and Connection Point Affinity ensure every transaction processed by the FedNow
Service is associated with a Connection Point on the originating side and a Connection Point on the
receiving side. Every message sent by the service is sent only to the queues associated with those
respective Connection Points.
This means for each transaction that originates from a Connection Point, the service will send subsequent
status notifications related to that initial transaction to the originating Connection Point. Conversely, on
the receiving side, one connection point will receive the first transaction notification and subsequent
status notifications.
Connection Point Affinity takes precedence over Connection Point Status. This means if a message is
sent from a Connection Point that is subsequently disconnected, the FedNow Service will continue to
send messages related to the original message to the disconnected Connection Point.
Notes:
•

Connection Point Affinity does not apply to pacs.002 messages sent after a transaction has been
completed/settled (e.g., a status notification from the receiving financial institution informing the
sending financial institution that the payment has been credited to the ultimate end customer).

•

Participants are allowed to have multiple connections open and are expected to make use of
concurrent connections to send and receive messages to meet their performance needs. For
participants that maintain multiple connection points, the expectation is that all connections and
queues, including readers and writers, are being monitored and maintained by the participant to
meet the FedNow Service requirements, including active monitoring of the connection and reestablishing the connection when necessary.

Security Protocols
The FedNow Service relies on multiple layered controls to ensure a high level of security for its
operations. The first layer of controls is provided by the FedLine Solutions networks. The details for these
controls are outside the scope of this document.
At the MQ protocol level, mutual Transport Layer Security (TLS) authentication is required. Participants
use FRS-issued client certificates to perform the mutual TLS authentication. Beyond authentication, the
client certificate is authorized for connection to a specific MQI channel, which is linked to a Connection
Point. Once connected to a channel, the client is granted GET and PUT rights to (and only to) the queues
associated with the respective Connection Point (as the Connection Point ID is part of the queue names).
The FedNow Service further validates that the entities referenced in the message (connecting party,
instructing agent) are allowed to use the queue on which the message was received.
An additional control is the cryptographic signature of messages, which is discussed further in
Signing and Verifying FedNow Service Messages. The FedNow Service validates the signature and the
association between the entity that sent the message and the key used to sign it.

FedNow Service Schemas
FedNow Service messages can carry a variety of payload types defined by the ISO 20022 standard.
Because of this, the FedNow Service message schemas include references to the respective ISO 20022
schemas that inform the versions of those schemas that are supported.
While the FedNow Service supports the specified ISO 20022 types, it does not support them as is.

Duplicate Messages from the FedNow Service
Participants must validate that all messages received from the FedNow Service have a unique message
ID in the Business Application Header. There is a potential for Participants to receive a duplicate
message for any message type from the FedNow Service. Participants must be prepared to recognize
and not process such messages in order to avoid processing the message more than once. The duplicate
message will be an identical copy of the previously delivered message, including the Business Application
Header3 and the message ID in the Business Application Header.

ensure the content of messages complies with their organization’s security requirements.
Message receivers are responsible for protecting their systems, including filtering, escaping and encoding
data before it is used.

FedNow Service Key Management and Exchange
The Federal Reserve Banks require messages exchanged with FedNow Service Participants to be
cryptographically signed using asymmetric public and private key pairs. Keys in a key pair are
mathematically related and are used to sign and validate the signature on messages. Digital signatures
help the receiver to verify that a message originated from the sender and the message content is
unchanged.
Participants or Service Providers (on their behalf) create public and private keys based on specifications
defined by the FedNow Service. The FedNow Service requires message signing keys to be valid for no
more than 364 days. Keys with an expiration date greater than 364 days are NOT accepted by the
service. Participants can opt to use shorter durations by defining an earlier key expiration date when
uploading keys to the FedNow Service.
The Federal Reserve Banks permit Participants to maintain multiple active keys and encourage
Participants to stagger key expiration times to avoid a scenario in which all keys expire at the same time
and there are no active keys.
For example, a Participant might register keys three to six months apart to provide adequate time to
update keys that expire. The Participant is responsible for ensuring an active signing key pair is
maintained within the FedNow Service to send messages through the service. If all keys expire, refer to
the instructions in Exchanging Participant Keys with the FedNow Service to reestablish an initial public
key using the FedNow interface.

Obtaining FedNow Service Public Keys
Messages originated by the FedNow Service are signed with FedNow Service internal private keys and
can be validated using the corresponding public key. FedNow Service key components include the key
and the key ID. Unlike Participant keys, FedNow Service keys do not have a key name.
Participants must first use the FedNow interface to retrieve the current public keys and initialize their
system with those public keys before sending and receiving messages to and from the FedNow Service.
The expiration of FedNow Service public keys is based on the calendar date using Eastern Standard
Time. Participants must maintain a list of active FedNow Service public keys. This is important because
the FedNow Service is bound to the same key lifespan requirements as Participants.
FedNow Service public keys are available on the FedNow interface or through its message functionality.
This functionality should help reduce operational overhead because it provides a programmatic process
to obtain an active set of FedNow Service public keys.
A Participant can request FedNow Service public keys by sending the GetAllFedNowActivePublicKeys
key message as defined by the schema. The response message is digitally signed and returns all FedNow
Service currently active keys. The response message should be validated by the receiver to ensure integrity.

FedNow Service Public Key Rotation
The FedNow Service periodically rotates its keys. When a new FedNow Service public key is available, a
broadcast message of admi.004 with event type “FNKY” is sent. The Participant can use this message to
update its cache of FedNow Service public keys. When Participants request an updated list of current
FedNow Service keys, the currently active set of keys will be provided to them.
The FedNow Service does not inform Participants when a FedNow Service public key has expired or
been revoked. For Participant applications, you might want to consider how you will keep the list of
FedNow Service public keys current without impacting the processing of messages.
When Participants receive the FedNow Service Broadcast message informing of a new key, it is a best
practice to check if any FedNow Service public keys have expired or were revoked and to adjust your
system accordingly. If the FedNow Service revokes all FedNow Service public keys at once, Participants
can retrieve the new FedNow Service public keys via the FedNow interface.

Example
As the Participant application processes messages coming from the FedNow Service, and an unknown
FedNow Service public key is encountered upon validating against the application's internal list of known
FedNow Service public keys, the application might want to invoke the action
GetAllFedNowActivePublicKeys to update the internal list of known FedNow Service public keys
and then retry the validation.
Because the FedNow Service may rotate in new keys to the set of known FedNow Service public keys,
the Participant applications should consider invoking GetAllFedNowActivePublicKeys periodically
(e.g., every 24 hours) to retrieve the current list of FedNow Service public keys and update the
application's internal list of known FedNow Service public keys.

1. Generate a SHA-512 Hash of the HTTP Message Body
The Content-Digest header contains a SHA-512 cryptographic digest of the HTTP message body.
Once generated, add the header as described in RFC 9530 and RFC 8941.
A generated sample Content-Digest value is shown below.

Sample Java code to generate an HTTP message body hash:
public void generateContentDigest(HttpHeaders headers, byte body[]) {
MessageDigest messageDigest = MessageDigest.getInstance("SHA-512");

byte[] digestValue = messageDigest.digest(body);
byte[] encodedDigestValue = Base64.getEncoder().encode(digestValue);

headers.set("Content-Digest", "sha-512=:" + new
String(encodedDigestValue) + ":");
}

2. Create Message Signature
HTTP messages are parsed into a signature base value using an established list of required signature
components. Signature components are either derived from HTTP parameters (URL,

sign the signature base. The signature and its signature inputs are then added to the Signature and
Signature-Inputs parameters.
All HTTP messages must provide valid Signature and Signature-Input headers in accordance with RFC
9421. The FedNow Service restricts this to a single signature and header/value pairs.

Verifying API Responses
All FedNow Service responses to Participant requests include a content digest and signature
(i.e., Content-Digest, Signature/Signature-Input headers). After receiving a response from the
FedNow Service, we encourage Participants to verify the response against the FedNow Service public
key in accordance with RFC 9421, Section 3.2, Verifying a Signature.
This section contains high-level instructions for verifying responses based on the requirements contained
in RFC 9421 and includes information specific to the FedNow Service implementation. Each step includes
sample code for your reference.

1. Validate the Content Digest
The content digest is a simple Base-64 encoded hash value. To validate a digest:
1. Extract the hash algorithm and hash value from the header value.
2. Generate a new hash value using the hash algorithm and response body.
3. Compare to the header's hash value against the generated value.
Notes:
•

A null/empty body is treated as an empty string when the FedNow Service generates the content
digest.

•

A body is expected to be UTF-8 encoded.

Appendix A: Configuring the MQ Client
IBM MQ is the primary means of communication between the FedNow Service and Participants.
Participants use the IBM MQ client library to connect remotely to FedNow Service MQ servers over the
FedLine Solutions closed user group networks.
The FedNow Service MQ environment operates as a single cluster across sites, allowing Participants to
connect to a server at any site. The MQ clustering software ensures messages are moved across to
nodes that have active listeners, when needed.
To PUT and/or GET messages from the FedNow Service queues, the Participant’s connecting application
needs to leverage an IBM MQ client library. The method of packing the library is unique to the application
environment. The minimum IBM MQ client Version.Release.Modification.Fixpack (V.R.M.F) for use with
the FedNow Service is 9.3.0.
Note: MQ server is not currently supported for FedNow Service connections.

Client (X.509) Certificate and Trust Chain
A trust store must be configured for use by the MQ client and/or application that contains the FRB CA root
and issuer signer certificates. This is mandatory for the first step in the mutual TLS handshake whereby
the MQ client will validate the FRS queue manager certificate via the signer certificates in the trust store
made available to the MQ client.
The MQ client and/or application must present an FRB CA signed (personal) certificate during the mutual
TLS handshake. This should exist in a keystore made available to the MQ client and application.

Note on MDBs and Activation Specifications
A client application using Message Driven Beans (MDBs) and Activation Specifications running in
WebSphere Application Server will face challenges receiving messages from the FedNow MQ queue
managers if the activation specification is configured to operate in Application Server Facilities (ASF)
mode. In ASF mode, an activation specification uses one thread to browse a queue for available
messages and when one is found it passes its id to another thread which uses another connection to get
the message.
The second connection could be connected to a different queue manager than the one that performed the
browse, and in that case the message will not be retrieved. To get around this behavior, the activation
specification must be configured to run in non-ASF mode.
